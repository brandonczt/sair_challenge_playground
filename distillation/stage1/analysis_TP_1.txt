# The Prover's Manual: Strategies for Equational Theory Implication

This manual outlines the most effective derivation strategies identified from successful proofs where an initial equation ($e_1$) implies a target equation ($e_2$).

## 1. Symmetry and Independence Analysis (The "Vanishing Variable" Strategy)
One of the most powerful moves is identifying variables that appear on only one side of an equation.
*   **Strategy:** If $e_1$ has the form $LHS(x, y) = RHS(y, x, z)$, and $z$ does not appear in the LHS, the RHS must evaluate to a value independent of $z$.
*   **Application:** In problems like `hard_0190`, the independence of $z$ implies that the operation $u * z$ is constant for all $u$ in the image. This often collapses the magma into a **Constant Magma** ($x * y = c$) or a **Projection Magma** ($x * y = x$ or $x * y = y$).

## 2. Fixed-Point and Identity Discovery
Successful proofs frequently identify "hidden" elements with special properties (identities or units).
*   **Strategy:** Group nested terms into a single representative $e$. For example, if $x = x * (y * (z * y))$, treat $w = y * (z * y)$ as a potential right identity.
*   **Axiom Pattern:** 
    1.  Define the set of right identities $R = \{w \mid \forall x, x * w = x\}$.
    2.  Use $e_1$ to show that certain combinations of elements (like $y * (z * y)$) are necessarily in $R$.
    3.  Prove that $R$ must be the entire set $M$ by showing that any $a \notin R$ leads to a contradiction (e.g., `hard_0117`).

## 3. Image and Range Constraints
Analyzing the "Image" of the binary operation often reveals structural limitations.
*   **Strategy:** If an equation implies $x * y = f(y * x)$, analyze the behavior of the mapping $f$ on the image of the operation.
*   **Structural Heuristic:** Check if the operation is an **involution** ($f(f(x)) = x$). In `hard_0056`, proving that left translations are involutions ($R^2 = id$) allowed the model to simplify deep nesting: $R(R(R(R(x)))) \to x$.

## 4. Normal Form Reductions: The "Projection" Magmas
Many "hard" problems collapse into one of three trivial structures. Proving the magma belongs to one of these classes is a shortcut to proving any $e_2$.
*   **Left-Zero Magma (First Projection):** $x * y = x$.
    *   *Sign:* $e_1$ often looks like $x = x * (\text{complex term})$.
*   **Right-Zero Magma (Second Projection):** $x * y = y$.
    *   *Sign:* $e_1$ often looks like $x = (\text{complex term}) * x$.
*   **Constant Magma:** $x * y = c$.
    *   *Sign:* $e_1$ forces the result to be independent of one or more variables through substitution.

## 5. Row/Column Consistency (The "A_x" Set)
For more complex structures, models use a "set-theoretic" approach to limit the possibilities of the operation table.
*   **Strategy:** Define $A_x$ as the set of all elements that, when multiplied by a specific set (like squares $S$), yield $x$. 
*   **Logic:** By showing $|A_x| \leq 1$, the model proves that the operation $x * y$ must result in a unique value $p_x$ regardless of $y$, effectively proving the magma is "row-constant" (`hard_0075`).

## 6. Derivation Directionality
*   **Success Pattern:** The most successful proofs rarely move from $e_1$ to $e_2$ directly via string substitution. Instead, they follow this flow:
    1.  **Extract Property:** From $e_1$, derive a global property (e.g., "all elements are right units").
    2.  **Define Structure:** State the resulting magma class (e.g., "this is a left-projection magma").
    3.  **Verify $e_2$:** Show that $e_2$ is an identity in that specific class of magmas.

## 7. Substitution Heuristics
When looking for contradictions or identities, the most effective substitutions are:
*   **Setting variables to "Identity Candidates":** If $e$ is a suspected identity, substitute $y=e$ or $z=e$.
*   **Diagonalization:** Setting $y=x$ to see if $x*x$ reveals a fixed point.
*   **Variable Elimination:** Substituting a complex term with a single variable to simplify the "shape" of the equation (e.g., replacing $(y * (y * z))$ with $e$).

# The Prover's Manual: Strategies for Equational Deduction

Successful proofs in this dataset rarely rely on brute-force substitution. Instead, they focus on identifying structural collapses (reducing the magma to a known type) or functional properties (treating operations as transformations).

## 1. The "Structural Collapse" Strategy
The most frequent path to success involves proving that Equation 1 forces the magma into a highly restrictive, well-known algebraic structure.
*   **Identification of "Zero" Magmas:**
    *   **Right Zero Magma ($x * y = y$):** Often identified when Equation 1 implies that every element (or a specific term) acts as a **universal left identity** ($e * x = x$).
    *   **Left Zero Magma ($x * y = x$):** Identified when Equation 1 implies that every element acts as a **universal right identity** ($x * e = x$).
*   **The Idempotency Bridge:** If you can prove $x * x = x$, it often serves as the "anchor" to simplify nested terms. 

## 2. Functional Analysis (Left/Right Translations)
Instead of viewing the equation as a relationship between elements, view it as a composition of functions.
*   **Translation Operators:** Define $L_a(b) = a * b$ and $R_a(b) = b * a$.
*   **Mapping to Identity:** If an equation can be written as $L_u \circ L_v \circ L_w = \text{id}$, and this holds for all $u, v, w$, it implies that all left translations $L$ are identical and are their own inverses (involutions).
*   **Constant Maps ($C_x$):** If an equation shows that a sequence of operations always results in $x$, then the composition of those translations is the constant map $C_x$. This often forces the image of the translation to be the singleton $\{x\}$.

## 3. The "Critical Pair" & Overlapping Term Heuristic
When an equation has a nested structure, evaluate a "super-term" in two different ways to force an identity.
*   **The "Two-Way Evaluation" Technique:**
    1.  Pick a complex term (e.g., $a * (b * (a * (b * c)))$).
    2.  Apply Equation 1 to the **entire** term.
    3.  Apply Equation 1 to an **inner** sub-term.
    4.  Equate the results to find a simpler ground truth (e.g., $a * b = a$).

## 4. Range and Image Arguments
When the equation involves many variables (like $x, y, z, w, u$), use the "Image" of the operation to restrict the magma.
*   **Image Inclusion:** Let $R = \{a * b \mid a, b \in M\}$. If Equation 1 shows $x * (\text{any product}) = x$, then every element in the range $R$ is a right identity.
*   **Exhaustion:** If you can show that the range $R$ is equal to the entire set $M$, then every element in the magma inherits the properties of the range (e.g., every element becomes a right identity).

## 5. Intermediate Lemma Checklist
Successful models frequently derive these "stepping stone" properties before attacking the target equation:
*   **Involution Property:** Does $f(f(x)) = x$? If so, any sequence of four identical operations ($f^4$) will return $x$.
*   **Square Property:** Is every element a "square"? If $x = (u * u)$, then a variable $z$ in Equation 1 can be replaced by any arbitrary element $w$ in the magma, significantly generalizing the axiom.
*   **Independence:** Does the value of $x * y$ depend only on $x$? If $x * y = x * z$ for all $y, z$, then the operation is purely a function of the left argument.

## 6. Simplification Direction
*   **LHS $\rightarrow$ RHS:** Best for "Zero Magma" proofs. Once $x * y = x$ is proven, simply evaluate the complex RHS of Equation 2 from the inside out.
*   **Normal Form:** Best for "Involutive" proofs. Simplify both sides of Equation 2 to a common functional form (e.g., both sides reduce to $f(x)$ or $x$).

## 7. Handling Complexity
*   **Variable Substitution:** If an equation holds for all $x, y, z$, substitute $y=x$ and $z=x$ immediately to find the "core" behavior of the operation (e.g., identifying $x * x = x$ or $x * (x * x) = x$).
*   **Fixed Points:** If $(R_x)^4 = C_x$, check if $x$ is the unique fixed point. This is often the key to proving the magma is a projection magma.

# The Prover’s Manual: Strategies for Equational Proofs

## 1. The "Ghost Variable" Heuristic (Identifying Constant Magmas)
If one side of the equation contains variables that do not appear on the other side, the operation is often a **Constant Product**.
*   **Pattern:** In `x * y = ((z * w) * x) * u`, variables `z`, `w`, and `u` are "free" on the RHS but absent on the LHS.
*   **Strategy:** 
    1.  Fix the common variables (like `x`) and vary the "ghost" variables (`z, w, u`).
    2.  Show that because the RHS evaluates to the same value regardless of the ghost variables, the product `x * y` must be independent of its inputs.
    3.  Define the magma as $a * b = c$ for some constant $c$.
    4.  Verify if the target equation holds when all products are replaced by $c$.

## 2. The Identity Set Construction
If an equation takes the form $\text{Term}(y, z, \dots) * x = x$, the magma contains **Left Identities**.
*   **Pattern:** `(y * (y * z)) * x = x` or `((y * z) * z) * x = x`.
*   **Strategy:**
    1.  Define a set $L = \{ \text{Term}(y, z, \dots) \mid y, z \in M \}$.
    2.  Note that every element in $L$ acts as a left identity ($e * x = x$).
    3.  **The "Surjectivity" Jump:** Use substitution to show that every element in the magma $M$ is actually in $L$. If $M = L$, then every element is a left identity, which forces the **Right-Projection Law**: $a * b = b$.
    4.  **Triviality Check:** If the uniqueness of the identity $e$ can be proven (e.g., $u$ and $v$ are identities, so $u = u * v = v$), try to prove the magma is a **Singleton** ($x = e$).

## 3. Transformation to Unary Functions (Functional Reduction)
If the proof suggests that one operand is ignored, reduce the binary operation to a unary function $f(x)$.
*   **Pattern:** `(y * x) * (y * x) = x`.
*   **Strategy:**
    1.  Define $f(x) = x * x$ (the "square" function).
    2.  Rewrite the axiom using $f$: e.g., $f(y * x) = x$.
    3.  Test for **Involution**: If $f(f(x)) = x$, the function is a bijection and its own inverse.
    4.  **Operand Elimination:** If $f(y * x) = x$, then $y * x = f^{-1}(x) = f(x)$. This proves the product depends only on the right operand ($y * x = f(x)$).
    5.  Simplify the target equation by replacing every $A * B$ with $f(B)$.

## 4. Logical Cancellation and Dependence
If an equation contains $z * x$ and $y * x$ in similar positions, attempt to prove the left operand is irrelevant.
*   **Pattern:** `x = (y * (y * x)) * (z * x)`.
*   **Strategy:**
    1.  **Prove Left Cancellation:** Show that $a * b = a * c \implies b = c$ by substituting into the axiom to get identical RHS forms for different variables.
    2.  **Equate Terms:** Use the axiom twice, once with $z$ and once with $y=z$, to show $(y * (y * x)) * (z * x) = (y * (y * x)) * (y * x)$.
    3.  **Apply Cancellation:** Conclude $z * x = y * x$. This confirms the magma operation is a function of the right argument only ($u * v = f(v)$).

## 5. Direction of Derivation: "Collapse then Verify"
Successful models rarely move linearly from LHS to RHS of the target equation. Instead, they follow a two-phase structure:
1.  **Phase 1: Reduction (LHS of Eq1 $\implies$ Structural Law).** Use the first equation to derive a "Normal Form" for the magma (e.g., $x*y=x$, $x*y=y$, or $x*y=c$).
2.  **Phase 2: Verification (Structural Law $\implies$ Eq2).** Substitute this simplified law into both sides of Equation 2 to show they reduce to the same term.

## 6. Target-Specific Substitution
When the target equation (Eq2) is complex (e.g., `x = (((x * y) * x) * x) * x`), models look for cycles.
*   **Strategy:** If the previous steps proved a projection law (like $a * b = b$), the proof is a simple step-by-step reduction of the nested terms from the inside out: $(x*y) \to y$, then $(y*x) \to x$, and so on.

## Summary of Common "Hidden" Properties
Models frequently derive these intermediate "Normal Forms" as stepping stones:
*   **Left-Projection:** $\forall a, b: a * b = a$
*   **Right-Projection:** $\forall a, b: a * b = b$
*   **Constant:** $\forall a, b, c, d: a * b = c * d$
*   **Involutive Right-Dependent:** $a * b = f(b)$ where $f(f(b)) = b$

## 1. Derivation Direction: The "Structural Classification" Approach
**Pattern:** Direct step-by-step substitution from `eq1` to `eq2` is almost never used.
**Strategy:** 
Instead of trying to manipulate the string of `eq1` into `eq2`, the most successful approach is to use `eq1` to classify the magma into a known rigid structure (e.g., Left Zero/Projection, Right Zero/Projection, Constant, or Involution). 
* **Phase 1 (Characterization):** Prove that `eq1` restricts the magma to a global rule (e.g., $\forall x, y \implies x * y = x$).
* **Phase 2 (Normal Form Reduction):** Apply this global rule to both the LHS and RHS of `eq2` independently. By evaluating `eq2` from the innermost parentheses outward, both sides predictably collapse into a trivial "Normal Form" (such as $x = x$ or $k = k$).

## 2. Axiom Application: The "Identity Bootstrapping" Technique
**Pattern:** Complex nested equations like $x = \text{[Nested Term]} * x$ strongly imply the existence of identity elements.
**Strategy:**
When facing deep nesting, models successfully dismantle the complexity using a three-step bootstrapping method:
1. **Construct a Local Identity:** Freeze the variables in the nested term by assigning them to arbitrary constants (e.g., $e = y_0 * (z_0 * z_0)$). Use `eq1` to prove $e$ acts as a left or right identity (e.g., $e * x = x$).
2. **Self-Application:** Evaluate the identity on itself (e.g., $e * e = e$) to collapse nested layers.
3. **Generalization (The Kill Shot):** Substitute $e$ back into the original `eq1` variables. Because $e$ simplifies the equation, it is frequently used to prove that *every* element in the magma is an identity element. This instantly proves the magma is a projection magma ($x * y = x$ or $x * y = y$).

## 3. Intermediate Lemmas: Set Theory and Surjectivity
**Pattern:** When direct substitution becomes computationally heavy, models pivot to deriving "hidden" set-theoretic properties as stepping stones.
**Strategy:**
* **Stabilizer Sets:** Define sets based on algebraic behavior, such as the set of all right identities $R = \{ r \mid \forall x, x * r = x \}$ or local left identities $I_x = \{ a \mid a * x = x \}$. The model then uses `eq1` to prove that complex sub-terms belong to these sets, eventually proving that the set is equal to the entire magma ($R = M$).
* **Surjectivity ($Im(*) = M$):** To simplify deeply nested terms (e.g., $y * z$), the model proves that every element in the magma can be expressed as a product. Once surjectivity is proven, a complex nested term like $(y * z)$ can be cleanly replaced by an arbitrary single variable $m$, drastically reducing the depth of the equation.

## 4. Structural Heuristics: Functional Embedding for Asymmetric Equations
**Pattern:** When an equation shows asymmetric variable dependencies (e.g., $x$ equals a term where $x$ only appears on the far right), algebraic rules act like hidden functions.
**Strategy:**
Instead of treating `*` purely as a binary operation, successful proofs treat partial evaluation as a function. 
* **Define a Map:** Introduce a hidden function, such as $\lambda(x)$ or $\pi(x)$, defining it as the output of the fixed operation (e.g., $a * b = \lambda(b)$).
* **Prove Functional Properties:** Use `eq1` to prove this function is an involution ($\lambda(\lambda(x)) = x$) or a bijection. This translates the messy algebraic equation into a clean functional composition problem, making it trivial to evaluate `eq2`.

## 5. Structural Heuristics: Proof by Contradiction on Cardinality
**Pattern:** Equations that "swallow" variables (where variables present on one side vanish on the other) often point to a Constant Magma ($x * y = k$).
**Strategy:**
To handle heavily variable-swallowing equations, the model avoids algebraic manipulation entirely and uses a proof by contradiction:
1. Assume the magma produces at least two distinct outputs, $p \neq q$.
2. Instantiate `eq1` twice, tracing the pathways that produce $p$ and $q$.
3. Cross-apply the global identity to force $p = q$, creating a contradiction. 
4. Conclude the magma only has one output ($x * y = k$), making `eq2` trivially true as both sides must equal $k$.

# The Prover's Manual: Strategies for Equational Meta-Analysis

This manual summarizes the most effective derivation strategies identified from successful proofs of $Eq_1 \implies Eq_2$.

### 1. The "Structural Collapse" Strategy (Axiom Application)
The most common path to success involves proving that the given axiom ($Eq_1$) forces the magma into a highly restrictive algebraic structure.
*   **Identification of Projections:** Models frequently test if $Eq_1$ reduces the magma to a **Left Zero Semigroup** ($x * y = x$) or a **Right Zero Semigroup** ($x * y = y$).
*   **Constant Operators:** Look for terms that act as "Global Identities." If $Eq_1$ is of the form $x = x * (\text{complex term})$, the complex term is often proved to be a **universal right identity**.
*   **The Singleton of Squares:** If the axiom contains $z * z$, check if all squares collapse to a single constant $c$ (e.g., Problem `hard_0070`). If $z * z = c$, then $c$ often acts as a pivot element for further reductions.

### 2. Derivation Direction: The "Normal Form" Bridge
Successful proofs rarely move linearly from the LHS of $Eq_2$ to its RHS. Instead, they follow a three-step sandwich structure:
1.  **Reduce $Eq_1$ to a Primitive Identity:** Simplify $Eq_1$ to its most basic functional form (e.g., $x * y = x$).
2.  **LHS Reduction:** Apply this primitive identity to the LHS of $Eq_2$ to find a "Normal Form" (usually a single variable like $x$ or $y$).
3.  **RHS Reduction:** Apply the same identity to the RHS of $Eq_2$ to see if it reaches the same Normal Form.

### 3. Intermediate Lemmas: The "Hidden Property" Stepping Stones
Models frequently derive these specific properties as intermediate steps before tackling $Eq_2$:
*   **Idempotency:** Proving $x * x = x$. This is often the first "break" in a complex equation (e.g., Problem `hard_0112`).
*   **Surjectivity/Injectivity of Translations:** Defining $R_a(b) = b * a$ (right translation) and $L_a(b) = a * b$ (left translation). If $R_a$ is shown to be the identity map or a constant map, the theory is solved.
*   **The "Right-Identity Set" ($R$):** Defining a set $R = \{r \mid x * r = x\}$ and proving that $R = M$ (the entire magma). This is a recurring theme in "Hard" problems.

### 4. Structural Heuristics: Breaking Down Nested Terms
When faced with complex nesting (e.g., `(((y * x) * y) * z) * x`), successful models use:
*   **Variable Substitution for Cancellation:** Substituting a complex sub-expression with a single variable to reveal a latent identity.
*   **Inner-Outward Evaluation:** In $Eq_2$, always resolve the innermost parentheses first using the identities derived from $Eq_1$.
*   **Double Evaluation of a "Witness" Term:** Constructing a specialized term $W$ and evaluating it two different ways (e.g., Problem `hard_0060`) to force an equality like $z * x = x$.

### 5. Summary of Successful "Primitive Theories"
If a proof can show $Eq_1$ implies one of the following, the implication for $Eq_2$ usually follows immediately:
*   **Left-Zero:** $x * y = x$
*   **Right-Projection:** $x * y = y$
*   **Self-Inverse/Commutative-like properties:** $(y * x) * y = x$

### 6. Logic Flow Template for High-Difficulty Problems
1.  **Assume** $Eq_1$ holds for all elements.
2.  **Isolate** a sub-term that must be a right/left identity.
3.  **Generalize** that every element produced by the operation is such an identity.
4.  **Conclude** the magma is a projection magma (Zero Semigroup).
5.  **Verify** $Eq_2$ by substituting the projection rule into both sides.

# Prover's Manual: High-Level Strategies for Equational Derivations

## 1. Axiom Application Patterns: The "Macroscopic" View
Successful proofs treat `Equation 1` (Eq1) not as a simple string-rewrite rule, but as a constraint that forces specific algebraic behaviors. 

*   **Constant Subterm Instantiation (The "e" Technique):** 
    Identify a complex subterm in Eq1 that relies entirely on universally quantified variables (e.g., `(y * z) * w` or `(a * b) * (b * b)`). By assigning arbitrary fixed values to these variables, you can define a constant element `e`. Substituting `e` back into Eq1 often immediately reveals it to be a **global left/right identity** or **absorbing element** (e.g., `x * e = x`).
*   **Evaluating Expressions in Two Ways (Divergent Evaluation):** 
    Construct a compound arbitrary expression like `(x * y) * (u * v)`. Use Eq1 to evaluate this expression down two different substitution paths. Equating the two resulting endpoints often forces a massive collapse in the operation's complexity (e.g., proving `y * x = u * v`, which means the operation is a constant function $A * B = c$).
*   **Functional Abstraction:** 
    Treat repeated variables as unary functions. For example, define a squaring map $f(u) = u * u$. Eq1 can often be rewritten as a functional equation (e.g., $f(y * x) = x$). Evaluating $f(f(x))$ can quickly prove that the map is an involution and bijective, allowing you to uniquely solve for terms.
*   **Set-Theoretic and Cardinality Arguments:** 
    Instead of pure algebra, use set theory. Define the image set of a partial operation (e.g., $S_x = \{ y * x \mid y \in M \}$). Use Eq1 to prove that sets for different $x$ are disjoint. In constrained systems, this forces $|S_x| = 1$, meaning the operation ignores its left argument entirely ($y * x = c_x$).

## 2. Intermediate Lemmas: Uncovering Hidden Properties
The most efficient proofs never attack `Equation 2` (Eq2) directly. They use Eq1 to derive hidden stepping-stone properties (lemmas) that define the magma. 
*   **Left/Right Projection (Zero Semigroups):** The most common successful hidden lemma is proving that the operation ignores one argument: $x * y = x$ (Left zero) or $x * y = y$ (Right zero).
*   **Idempotence ($x * x = x$):** Once an identity element $e$ is found, substituting $e$ alongside $x$ frequently yields idempotence. This is a critical property for destroying infinite nested loops.
*   **Property Propagation (The "Infection" Lemma):** If you can prove that *one* generated element acts as a left unit, substitute that unit back into Eq1 to prove that *all* elements in the magma must also be left units. If $L$ is the set of left units, show $L = M$.

## 3. Derivation Direction: The "Global Reduction" Approach
*   **Avoid LHS-to-RHS string rewriting:** Attempting to syntactically transform the LHS of Eq2 into the RHS using Eq1 is a recipe for combinatorial explosion and nested loops.
*   **The "Normal Form" Pipeline:** 
    1. Parse Eq1.
    2. Deduce the global structure of the magma (e.g., "This is a Left Zero Semigroup" or "This is a Constant Function").
    3. Take Eq2, and evaluate the LHS and RHS *independently* using the newly discovered global rules.
    4. Both sides will trivially collapse to a Normal Form (e.g., $x = x$ or $c = c$), completing the proof.

## 4. Structural Heuristics: Breaking Down Complexity
When the model finally addresses the target Eq2, it uses specific heuristics to melt away deeply nested terms.
*   **Inside-Out Collapse:** When evaluating the heavily nested terms of Eq2, apply the derived intermediate lemmas to the *innermost* parentheses first. 
    *   *Example:* If the lemma is $u * v = v$, a terrifying term like $y * (y * (z * (w * x)))$ cascades elegantly: $w * x \to x$, then $z * x \to x$, then $y * x \to x$, collapsing the entire expression to just $x$ in a few steps.
*   **Variable Separation via Identity:** If Eq1 has a single $x$ on one side and a complex mix of $x, y, z$ on the other, intentionally substitute $y = x$ or $z = x$ to force identical inputs to interact. This separates variables and usually triggers a collapse that reveals how the operation behaves under uniform inputs.
*   **Symmetry Exploitation:** Swap $x$ and $y$ in Eq1 to generate a mirrored equation. Substituting the mirrored equation back into the original often cancels out nested layers and exposes pseudo-commutative properties.

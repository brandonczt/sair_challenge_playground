This "Prover's Manual" summarizes the high-level strategies and logical structures identified from successful proofs in the Equational Theories challenge. The analysis reveals that the most effective derivations often bypass complex term rewriting in favor of identifying **structural collapses** into known degenerate algebraic structures.

### **The Prover’s Manual: Strategies for Equational Meta-Analysis**

#### **1. Identifying Structural Collapses (The "Degeneracy" Search)**
The most frequent and successful strategy is proving that `equation1` forces the magma into a highly restricted form. Models rarely stay in the "general magma" space if they can prove the system collapses into:
*   **Left-Zero Magma ($x * y = x$):** The most common target. Proven by showing every element is a right identity or that the operation ignores the right operand.
*   **Right-Zero Magma ($x * y = y$):** Proven by showing every element is a left identity.
*   **Constant Magma ($x * y = q$):** Proven by showing the product is independent of both operands.
*   **Involutionary Squares ($f(f(x)) = x$):** Useful when the equation involves nested squares or symmetric term structures.

#### **2. Axiom Application Patterns: The "Identity Hunter"**
Successful proofs typically prioritize finding an identity element ($e$) or a set of identities ($S$).
*   **The Specialization Trick:** Substituting variables to isolate a specific term. 
    *   *Example:* If $x = x * (\text{complex term})$, define $e = \text{complex term}$ to show $x * e = x$.
*   **Universal Right/Left Unit Property:** If the model can show $x = x * \text{Term}(y, z)$, and $\text{Term}(y, z)$ can span the whole magma or contains an arbitrary variable, it concludes $x * y = x$.
*   **Image Analysis:** Models often analyze the range of the operation. If the image of a sub-expression covers all of $M$, and that sub-expression acts as an identity, then all of $M$ consists of identities.

#### **3. Derivation Direction: The "Model-Theoretic" Leap**
Unlike standard algebraic proofs that move linearly from $LHS \to RHS$, successful proofs in this dataset often follow a three-step non-linear path:
1.  **Reduction:** Transform `eq1` into a primitive property (e.g., $x * y = x$ or $x * x = e$).
2.  **Generalization:** State that all magmas satisfying `eq1` belong to a specific class (e.g., "This is a left-zero semigroup").
3.  **Verification:** Plug that class-wide property into `eq2` to show it holds trivially.

#### **4. Intermediate Lemmas (Hidden Properties)**
Successful models frequently derive these "stepping stone" properties:
*   **Surjectivity of Translations:** Proving that the map $R_s(u) = u * s$ is surjective allows the model to pick an element $u$ such that $u * e = e$, which is critical for proving $e$ is a global identity.
*   **Square Equality:** Proving $x * x = y * y$ (all squares coincide) is a powerful intermediate step to simplify complex terms into a single constant.
*   **Operand Independence:** Proving $x * y = f(x)$ or $x * y = g(y)$. Once the model shows one operand is irrelevant, complex nested terms in `eq2` collapse instantly.

#### **5. Structural Heuristics for Nested Terms**
How models handle terms like `x = (x * ((y * y) * x)) * x`:
*   **The "Inside-Out" Substitution:** Substitute the entirety of `eq1` back into itself. If `eq1` is $x = \text{Op}(x)$, replacing the $x$ inside $\text{Op}$ with $\text{Op}(x)$ often reveals $x * y = x * z$.
*   **Variable Erasure:** If a variable (like $z$ or $w$) appears on only one side of `eq1` or within a term that is equated to a fixed variable, the model assumes that variable’s value is irrelevant to the outcome, leading to the "Constant Product" theory.
*   **The Involution Shortcut:** If a term is repeated (like $(y*x)*(y*x)$), the model defines a function $f(a) = a*a$ and treats the problem as a functional equation problem rather than a magma problem.

#### **6. Summary Checklist for Proving $E_1 \implies E_2$**
1.  **Can I find a Right/Left Identity?** (Substitute to make a term act as $e$).
2.  **Is this a Projection Magma?** (Check if $x * y = x$ or $x * y = y$ holds).
3.  **Are all squares equal?** (Set $x=y$ and see if $x*x$ becomes a constant).
4.  **Does the RHS of $E_2$ simplify under these assumptions?** (Usually, it collapses to $x$).

This strategy of **"Reducing to Class, then Verifying Class"** is significantly more efficient than brute-force substitution and characterizes almost all "hard" problem successes.

### **The Prover’s Manual: Strategies for Equational Implications**

#### **1. Axiom Application Patterns: Collapsing to Constants**
The most frequent path to success in complex "hard" problems is proving that the axiom forces the magma into a **degenerate state**. 
*   **Constant Magmas ($x * y = c$):** Models often look for "floating" variables on one side of the equation. In `hard_0190` ($x * y = (y * x) * z$), the presence of $z$ on the RHS (with no corresponding $z$ on the LHS) implies the product must be independent of $z$.
*   **Zero-Element Magmas:** 
    *   **Left-Zero ($x * y = x$):** If an equation like $x = x * (\text{complex term})$ holds, and the complex term can span the entire magma, the operation collapses to the left-parent.
    *   **Right-Zero ($x * y = y$):** Proved by showing every element $z$ acts as a left identity ($z * x = x$), often by deriving that a specific term $e$ is a left identity and then showing $e * z = z$.

#### **2. Derivation Direction: The "Structural Collapse"**
Successful proofs rarely move linearly from the LHS of `eq1` to the RHS of `eq2`. Instead, they follow a two-phase structure:
1.  **Phase 1: Global Characterization (Top-Down):** Analyze `eq1` to identify a global algebraic property (e.g., "all rows are constant," "the image is a singleton," or "the operation is an involution").
2.  **Phase 2: Identity Verification (Bottom-Up):** Apply the discovered property to both sides of `eq2` to show they reduce to the same "Normal Form" (usually a single variable $x$ or a global constant $K$).

#### **3. Intermediate Lemmas: The "Hidden" Properties**
Models frequently derive these "stepping stone" properties to bridge the gap between complex nested terms:
*   **The Singleton Image Lemma:** If $x * y = f(x, z, w, \dots)$, and the variables $z, w$ are arbitrary, then the image of the left-multiplication map $L_x$ must be a singleton. 
*   **The Left/Right Stabilizer:** Identifying sets like $S = \{y * (y * z)\}$ and proving $S$ is a singleton. If $x * s = x$ for all $s \in S$, and $S$ is the whole magma, then $x * y = x$.
*   **Injectivity and Involutions:** In problems like `hard_0056`, models prove that right-multiplication $R_w$ is injective. If $R_{s_0}$ is an involution ($R_{s_0}^2 = \text{id}$), it allows for "canceling" layers of nested applications.

#### **4. Structural Heuristics: Handling Nesting**
When faced with deeply nested terms (e.g., $x = (y * (y * z)) * (w * x)$), successful strategies include:
*   **Variable Substitution for Independence:** Replace sub-terms with $u$ or $v$ to see if the equation remains valid. If $x = (\text{Term A}) * (\text{Term B})$ and Term A can vary while $x$ and Term B stay fixed, Term A must be a "null" operator (like a left identity).
*   **Row/Column Constant Analysis:** Treat the Cayley table as a set of rows. If $x * y = x * z$, then "row $x$ is constant." If all row constants $k_x$ are equal, the magma is constant.
*   **Function Abstraction:** Convert the magma operation into functional notation, e.g., $x * y = f(x)$. This simplifies $x * (y * z)$ to $f(x)$, instantly collapsing nested structures.

#### **5. Summary of Successful Logic Flow**
1.  **Identify "Free" Variables:** Variables appearing on only one side of the axiom usually signal that the operation is constant relative to those variables.
2.  **Test for Identity elements:** Check if any sub-expression in `eq1` forces an element to behave as a left or right identity.
3.  **Check Image Size:** Determine if the range of the operation is the whole set $M$ or a single point $\{c\}$.
4.  **Simplify `eq2`:** Once the "behavior" (Left-Zero, Right-Zero, or Constant) is identified, evaluate `eq2` under that regime. If both sides match, the implication is TRUE.

### 1. Axiom Application Patterns: The "Collapsing" Technique
The most effective strategy in successful proofs is identifying when an axiom forces the binary operation into a degenerate or highly restricted state.

*   **Independence of Variables:** If one side of an equation contains a variable (e.g., $z$) that does not appear on the other side (e.g., $x * y = ((z * w) * x) * u$), the expression must be independent of that variable. 
    *   *Strategy:* Show that for any $z_1, z_2$, the output remains the same, implying the operation $x * y$ is actually a function of fewer arguments (e.g., $f(x)$ or a constant $c$).
*   **Translation Analysis:** View the operation as a set of maps. In $x = (y * (y * z)) * (w * x)$, successful proofs treated $\lambda_a(b) = a * b$ as a left translation.
    *   *Strategy:* If $\lambda_{f(y,z)} \circ \lambda_w = \text{id}$, then $\lambda_w$ must be bijective. If this holds for all $w$, and the inverse is independent of certain variables, it often forces all translations to be identical ($\lambda_a = \lambda_b$).

### 2. Derivation Direction: Reduction to "Canonical Magmas"
While standard algebraic proofs often move LHS $\to$ RHS, these high-difficulty proofs almost exclusively move **Axiom $\to$ Model Class $\to$ Target**.

*   **Identify the Model Class:** Successful models first proved that `eq1` restricts the magma to a specific known type:
    *   **Left-Zero Magma:** $x * y = x$
    *   **Constant Magma:** $x * y = c$
    *   **Permutation Magma:** $x * y = f(y)$ where $f^2 = \text{id}$
*   **Verification:** Once the model class is identified, "proving" `eq2` becomes a trivial matter of substituting the simplified rule (e.g., $x * y = x$) into both sides of `eq2` and showing they collapse to the same term.

### 3. Intermediate Lemmas: "Hidden" Properties
Models frequently derived specific "stepping stone" properties that act as catalysts for the final proof:

*   **Right Identity Discovery:** In many cases (e.g., $x = x * ((y * z) * (z * z))$), the model identifies a specific nested term that must act as a right identity ($e$). 
    *   *Lemma:* "There exists an $e$ such that $x * e = x$."
*   **Involution Property:** In proofs where $x * y = f(y)$, the models almost always derive $f(f(x)) = x$ as the primary lemma.
*   **The "Singleton Image" Lemma:** For constant magmas, the model proves that the image of the operation is a singleton set by showing $f(x) = f(f(f(z)))$ for all $x, z$.

### 4. Structural Heuristics: Handling Nested Terms
Complex nested terms are the primary obstacle. Successful strategies include:

*   **The "Inner Term Map" Coverage:** If an equation like $x = x * (y * (x * z))$ holds, models argue that the "inner term" $(y * (x * z))$ must be able to evaluate to any element in the magma to satisfy the identity for all $x$. This is used to prove the magma is a left-zero semigroup.
*   **Variable Substitution for Cancellation:** Replace variables with expressions that simplify the axiom. 
    *   *Example:* If $x * e = x$ is known, substitute $z = e$ into a more complex axiom to strip away layers of nesting.
*   **Iterative Independence:** In equations like $x * y = ((z * w) * x) * u$, models systematically replaced variables (e.g., replacing $w$ with $z$) to show that sub-expressions collapse step-by-step until only one variable remains.

### 5. Summary of Successful Logic Flow
1.  **Analyze `eq1` for "unbalanced" variables** (variables appearing on only one side).
2.  **Determine if the operation is "Right-Stable" or "Left-Stable"** (does the result change if you change the second or first argument?).
3.  **Define the operation's functional form** (e.g., $x * y = c$, $x * y = x$, or $x * y = f(y)$).
4.  **Check for Involution** ($f^2 = \text{id}$) by re-substituting the functional form into `eq1`.
5.  **Simplify `eq2`** using the derived functional form. If both sides match, the implication is TRUE.

### **The Prover's Manual: Strategies for Equational Theories**

#### **1. Axiom Application Patterns: Identifying "Structural Collapsers"**
Successful proofs often identify when an equation forces the magma into a highly restricted algebraic structure. Look for these three primary "collapsers":

*   **The Right/Left Identity Generator:** If `equation1` contains a term that acts as an identity (e.g., $x = E(y, z, \dots) * x$), define that term as $e$. If $e$ is independent of $x$, it is a **Left Identity**. 
    *   *Strategy:* Substitute $y=e$ back into the original equation to see if the identity property propagates to all elements.
*   **The Constant Map (Zero Semigroup):** If one side of `equation1` contains a variable not present on the other side (e.g., $x*y = (y*x)*z$), the operation must be independent of that variable.
    *   *Strategy:* Conclude the operation is "constant" ($x * y = c$) or "projection-based" ($x * y = x$ or $x * y = y$).
*   **Involutionary Mapping ($f^2 = id$):** If the equation reduces to $x = f(f(x))$, the operation is a bijection (specifically an involution).
    *   *Strategy:* Use the property that $f$ is a bijection to show $y * x$ depends only on $x$, effectively reducing the operation to a unary function $f(x)$.

#### **2. Derivation Direction: The "Model-Based Reduction"**
Unlike standard algebraic proofs that move LHS $\to$ RHS, successful "hard" proofs typically follow a **Reduction to Normal Form** via a known variety:
1.  **Analyze Equation 1** to identify which variety of magma it defines (e.g., Right Zero Semigroup, Left Projection Magma, Constant Magma, or Boolean Group).
2.  **State the "Law of the Variety"** (e.g., "Every element is a right identity, so $x * y = x$").
3.  **Apply the Law** to both sides of Equation 2. If both sides reduce to the same variable or term under this law, the implication is true.

#### **3. Intermediate Lemmas: The "Hidden" Properties**
Models frequently derive these "stepping stone" properties to bridge the gap between complex nested terms:
*   **Image Inclusion ($Im(*) = M$):** If every $x$ can be written as a product (from `equation1`), then every element in the set is a "product." This is crucial for proving that properties applying to "any product $a*b$" actually apply to "all elements $x$."
*   **Column/Row Disjointness:** If $A * b = x$ and $A * b = x'$, then $x = x'$. This logic is used to prove that columns ($w * x$) must be singletons, leading to the conclusion that $a * x = f(x)$ (Right-Constant).
*   **The Power of Substitution:** A common successful move is substituting an identity element $e$ into the variable slots of the original axiom to "peel away" layers of nesting.

#### **4. Structural Heuristics: Breaking Down Nested Terms**
When faced with deeply nested expressions (e.g., $x = ((y * z) * w) * u$), models use the following:
*   **The "Block" Substitution:** Treat a complex sub-term (like $((y * z) * w)$) as a single entity $S$. Prove that any $s \in S$ has a specific property (like being a left identity).
*   **The Variable Independence Test:** If a variable appears on the RHS but not the LHS, evaluate the "limit" of the expression. If $x = \text{Term}(y, z, x)$, then $\text{Term}$ must be the identity function regardless of $y$ and $z$.
*   **Unary Simplification:** Replace the binary operation with a unary function $f(x)$ if the proof shows the operation is right-constant ($x * y = f(y)$). This collapses nested magmatic multiplication into functional composition ($f(f(f(x)))$), which is much easier to simplify.

#### **Summary of Successful Proof Logic Flow:**
1.  **Assume** Equation 1.
2.  **Isolate** a sub-term that acts as a partial identity or constant.
3.  **Generalize** that property to the entire domain (e.g., "All elements are right identities").
4.  **Define** the resulting simple law (e.g., $x * y = x$).
5.  **Simplify** Equation 2 using that law to show LHS = RHS.

# 📖 The Prover's Manual: Strategies for Equational Theories

## 1. Derivation Direction: Theory Reduction over Direct Rewriting
**Finding:** Successful models rarely attempt a direct syntactic rewrite from `Equation 1` to `Equation 2` (e.g., manipulating the LHS of Eq2 until it looks like the RHS using Eq1). 
**Strategy: Global Normal Form Derivation.**
Instead of local term rewriting, successful proofs treat `Equation 1` as a constraint on the entire algebraic structure (the magma). The goal is to collapse the theory into a well-known, trivial magma class. 
* **The Workflow:** 
  1. Extract structural constraints from Eq 1.
  2. Prove the magma belongs to a strict class (e.g., *Left Projection* $x * y = x$, *Right Zero Semigroup* $x * y = y$, or *Constant Magma* $x * y = c$).
  3. Evaluate the LHS and RHS of `Equation 2` independently under this new, simplified global rule until they both reduce to the identical Normal Form.

## 2. Structural Heuristics: Deconstructing Complex Terms
When faced with deeply nested and intimidating equations, successful models deploy specific heuristics to break them down:
* **The "Identity-Generator" Block:** Models look for a nested sub-expression that outputs the original variable. 
  * *Example (hard_0197):* In $(((y * x) * y) * z) * x = x$, the entire block $e = (((y * x) * y) * z)$ acts as a left identity for $x$.
* **Absorption by Arbitrary Variables:** If a sub-term containing a universally quantified variable (like $z$) acts as an identity or evaluates to a specific element, the model deduces that the operation *must* discard/absorb that variable. This is a primary trigger for discovering projection maps ($x * y = x$).
* **Functional Abstraction:** Treating the binary operation as a set of unary functions.
  * *Translation Maps:* Defining $L_y(u) = y * u$ or $R_y(u) = u * y$ to study surjectivity/injectivity (seen in `hard_0150` and `hard_0046`).
  * *Squaring Maps:* Defining $f(u) = u * u$. In `hard_0052`, defining $f(y*x) = x$ immediately revealed that $f$ is an involution ($f(f(x)) = x$) and therefore bijective.

## 3. Axiom Application Patterns: Effective Transformations
The models utilize three highly effective algebraic transformations to force the magma to reveal its properties:
* **Variable Unification (Collapsing):** Setting multiple distinct variables to the same variable (e.g., $y = x$, $z = x$). This collapses nested trees into repeated operations (squares) and is the most reliable way to force idempotence ($x * x = x$).
* **Recursive Subterm Substitution (Bootstrapping):** A brilliant trick where the model substitutes a single variable with *an entire nested instance* of a known identity. 
  * *Example (hard_0183):* To simplify $x * (y * (x * z)) = x$, the model substitutes $z = y * (x * w)$, creating a self-similar fractal term $x * (y * [x * (y * (x * w))])$ which instantly collapses recursively.
* **Image and Surjectivity Set Analysis:** Instead of working with individual variables, the model works with the image of the set $M$. 
  * *Example (hard_0102):* Proving that the set of right identities $S_3$ is generated by arbitrary variables, meaning the image of the operation spans the whole set $M$, concluding that *every* element is a right identity.

## 4. Intermediate Lemmas: The "Hidden" Stepping Stones
Successful proofs rarely jump straight to the final magma class. They systematically hunt for specific intermediate lemmas. If you are building a prover, prioritize searching for these hidden properties in this order:
1. **Local Identities:** "Does there exist *any* term $e$ such that $e * x = x$?"
2. **Global Identities:** "Can I prove that $e$ is independent of its inner variables, meaning *every* element in the magma is an identity?" (Seen in `hard_0116` and `hard_0111`).
3. **Idempotence ($x * x = x$):** The ultimate bridge lemma. Once a model proves idempotence, it can usually collapse the remaining nested structures into Left/Right projections.
4. **Commutativity of specific subterms:** Proving that an expression is independent of one of its variables (e.g., proving $k(x)$ relies only on $x$ and not on $y$, as seen in `hard_0070`).

- [ ] **Don't** try to syntactically rewrite Eq1 into Eq2. 
- [ ] **Do** search for $x * x = x$ (Idempotence) by unifying variables.

- [ ] **Do** check if the operation can be reduced to Left Projection ($x * y = x$), Right Projection ($x * y = y$), or a Constant ($x * y = c$).

## 1. Axiom Application Patterns (Algebraic Transformations)
Successful proofs rely on specific, powerful algebraic transformations rather than random substitutions. 

*   **Subterm Abstraction (The "Black Box" Pattern):** Whenever an equation features a complex term multiplying a variable to yield that same variable (e.g., `x = T(y,z) * x`), the prover isolates `T(y,z)` as a "black box" left identity. 
    *   *Example:* In `x = (y * (y * z)) * x`, the term `(y * (y * z))` is abstracted as a left identity `w`.
*   **Bootstrapping via Self-Substitution:** Once a specific identity element `e` is discovered, the models immediately substitute `e` back into the general variables of the axiom to strip away complexity. 
    *   *Example:* After proving `e` is a left identity, substituting `y=e` and `z=w` into `e * (e * w)` reduces to `e * w = w`, elegantly proving that *every* element `w` is a left identity.
*   **Operator / Functional Translation:** For highly nested equations, successful provers stop treating `*` purely as an algebraic symbol and translate it into a functional map. 
    *   *Example:* Defining left-multiplication $L_a(x) = a * x$. If `a * (w * x) = x`, then $L_a \circ L_w = \text{id}$. This transitions the problem to functional properties like bijections and involutions, which are much easier to reason about algebraically.

## 2. Derivation Direction (The "Semantic Detour")
**Rule of thumb: Never attempt a direct LHS-to-RHS syntactic derivation.** 
Almost 100% of the successful proofs abandon the direct manipulation of `Equation 1` to strictly match the syntax of `Equation 2`. 

*   **Step 1: Discover the Global Variety.** The prover uses `Equation 1` to prove the magma belongs to a highly restrictive class. The most common targets are:
    *   **Right Zero Semigroup:** $a * b = b$
    *   **Left Zero Semigroup:** $a * b = a$
    *   **Constant Magma:** $a * b = c$
    *   **Involution Magma:** $a * b = f(b)$ where $f(f(x)) = x$
*   **Step 2: Independent Evaluation to Normal Form.** Once the global property is established (e.g., $a * b = b$), the prover takes `Equation 2` and simplifies both the LHS and RHS independently. If both sides collapse to the exact same "Normal Form" (usually just `x` or `c`), the proof is complete.

## 3. Intermediate Lemmas (Hidden Stepping Stones)
The models frequently derive "hidden" lemmas as stepping stones to unlock the global structure.

*   **The "One Identity Generates All" Lemma (Identity Propagation):** 
    If `Equation 1` proves the existence of *some* right/left identity, the prover sets up a lemma to show that the operation generates the entire space. By feeding the known identity back into the generating term, they prove that *all* elements in the magma are identities.
*   **The "Missing Variable" Constant Lemma:** 
    If an expression evaluates to a result that is completely independent of one of its input variables (e.g., `(y * x) * z` does not dictate the value of `z` on the other side of the equation), the prover deduces a lemma that the operation must yield a constant, or that the specific variable is absorbed.

## 4. Structural Heuristics
How do the models break down intimidating, deeply nested terms?

*   **"Fix and Vary" Quantifier Manipulation:** Instead of universally quantifying all variables at once, the prover intentionally *fixes* the outer variable (e.g., $x$) and considers the image of the subterms by *varying* the inner variables ($y, z$). This frames the nested term as a set (e.g., $T_x = \{y * (x * z)\}$), allowing the prover to apply set-theoretic boundary conditions.
*   **Inside-Out Collapse for Target Equations:** When verifying `Equation 2`, successful models rigidly apply the "Normal Form" rule from the innermost parentheses outward. 
    *   *Example:* To evaluate `(((x * y) * x) * x) * x` under a Right Zero rule ($a * b = b$):
        1. `x * y` becomes `y` -> `((y * x) * x) * x`
        2. `y * x` becomes `x` -> `(x * x) * x`
        3. `x * x` becomes `x` -> `x * x`
        4. Final result: `x`.

# **Category Master Report: Equational Theory Derivation (FN)**

This report synthesizes multiple batch analyses concerning **False Negatives (FN)**—cases where a logical implication is true, but the model fails to produce a valid proof, prematurely abandons the search, or substitutes rigorous derivation with "semantic bluffing."

---

### **1. Core Pillars (Successful Strategy Groupings)**
To overcome the failure to derive, the following "Core Pillars" must be enforced to transition the model from a "human-like" mathematical writer to an **Automated Theorem Prover (ATP)**:

*   **Syntactic Rigor (The Substitution Chain):** Success is found only when the model treats the problem as a pure string-rewriting task. Every step must be an explicit equation $A=B$ with a documented mapping (e.g., $x \gets [term]$).
*   **Variable Unification & Shadowing:** The most powerful tool for simplifying complex axioms is "self-unification"—setting $x=y$, $y=z$, or substituting a variable with a complex term already present in the axiom to force nested patterns to collapse.
*   **Intermediate Lemmatization:** High-impact proofs are rarely direct. They require "stepping stones" (e.g., first proving idempotence $x*x=x$, commutativity $x*y=y*x$, or the existence of a constant $c$) before attacking the target equation.
*   **Bidirectional Convergence:** Simultaneously working forward from Equation 1 (expansion) and backward from Equation 2 (un-simplification) to find a common intermediate term.

---

### **2. Critical Failure Points (Hallucinations & Pitfalls)**
*   **Semantic Hand-wavy Shortcuts:** Models often cite "known theorems," "finite magma checks," or properties like "left-zero semigroups" and "surjectivity" to avoid doing the actual algebra.
*   **The "Singleton" Trap (Complexity Bias):** When an equation looks daunting, models often hallucinate that the theory "collapses" into a 1-element magma ($x=y$) to "zero out" the difficulty, rather than finding the algebraic path.
*   **Premature Abandonment (Depth 0-2):** Models typically give up after 2-3 trivial substitutions if a result isn't immediate, pivoting to verbal justifications instead of deeper search.
*   **Expansion Loops & Term Bloat:** Falling into a "local optimum" where terms are expanded infinitely (e.g., $x \to x*x \to (x*x)*(x*x)$) without ever applying a reduction step to shrink the expression.

---

### **3. Rank by Impact (High-Frequency Patterns)**
1.  **Metalogic Displacement (Highest Impact):** Replacing symbolic manipulation with descriptive English (e.g., "The surjectivity of $L_x$ implies...").
2.  **Substitution Timidity:** Only attempting simple variable swaps ($x \to y$) while ignoring "machine-like" complex substitutions (e.g., $x \to (y*z)*y$) necessary for unification.
3.  **Missing "Expand-and-Collapse" Bridges:** Failing to intentionally inflate an equation to trigger a latent axiom pattern that allows for a massive simplification.

---

### **4. Actionable Logic (Derivation Rules & Triggers)**

#### **Derivation Rules (To enforce in prompts):**
*   **Rule of 10:** You must perform at least 10 distinct, numbered algebraic substitutions before concluding a proof is unreachable.
*   **The Identity Trigger:** If the target equation contains a variable not in the source, your primary sub-goal is to derive an "absorbing element" or "constant transformation" (e.g., $x*f(y)=x$).
*   **The 15-Symbol Ceiling:** If an intermediate term exceeds 15 symbols, you must **[BACKTRACK]**. Discard the branch and attempt a different, non-obvious substitution.
*   **Symmetry Test:** For every derived lemma $x = f(x, y)$, immediately test the results of swapping $x \leftrightarrow y$.

#### **Refutation Heuristics (When to stop proving and start disproving):**
*   **Counter-Model Trigger:** If the axiom is satisfied by a specific 2-element magma that violates the target equation, immediately stop the proof and provide the operation table for that magma.
*   **The $x=y$ Constraint:** Only declare a "Trivial/Singleton Magma" if you can provide a direct syntactic chain proving $x=y$ for any arbitrary $x$ and $y$. Do not assume triviality based on "complexity bias."

---

### **Core Pillars (Key Logical Patterns)**
1.  **Deep Syntactic Rewriting (Forward & Backward):** Successful strategies require moving beyond 2–4 steps. This involves "Term Bloat"—purposely expanding a variable $x$ into a complex expression from the axiom’s RHS—to create the structural "surface area" needed for cancellation or transformation into Equation 2.
2.  **Strategic Lemma Generation:** Instead of jumping to the final equation, successful derivations identify intermediate "fixed points" or "local identities" (e.g., proving $x*x = x$ or $x*y = x*(z*z)$).
3.  **Variable Shadowing & Functional Range:** Recognizing that if a variable $z$ appears only on one side of an equation, it represents an arbitrary element in the image of the magma. This allows treating complex terms as "blobs" to isolate the behavior of other variables.
4.  **Bi-directional Convergence:** Transforming Eq1 forward and Eq2 backward simultaneously to find a "Bridge Term" where the two structures unify.

---

### **Critical Failure Points (Hallucinations & Pitfalls)**
1.  **The Triviality Trap (Model-Theoretic Bailout):** Models prematurely conclude that Equation 1 is so restrictive it forces the magma to be a singleton ($|M|=1$). They stop the proof there, assuming "everything is true in a 1-element set," which fails formal syntactic verification.
2.  **Semantic Classification Bias:** Models attempt to classify the structure as a "Quasigroup," "Boolean Group," or "Left-Zero Magma" rather than performing algebraic substitutions. They substitute formal logic with prose-based "meta-arguments."
3.  **Implicit Property Hallucination:** Incorrectly assuming associativity or commutativity. Models often "clean up" nested parentheses (e.g., moving from $a*(b*c)$ to $(a*b)*c$) without a derivation, which is an illegal move in raw equational theory.
4.  **Search Ceiling (The 3-Step Wall):** Models hit a "complexity ceiling" after 3 substitutions. If the target isn't visible, they stop calculating and resort to generic claims like "it can be shown that..." or provide empty/truncated responses.

---

### **Refutation Heuristics (Disproving Implication)**
1.  **Non-Triviality Check:** Before concluding "True" via collapse, attempt to construct a size 2 or 3 magma table. If a 2-element "Left-Zero" ($a*b=a$) or "Right-Zero" ($a*b=b$) table satisfies Eq1 but violates Eq2, the implication is false.
2.  **Variable Decoupling:** if Eq2 requires a variable $z$ to be eliminated that is "trapped" in a non-cancelable position in Eq1, the implication likely fails unless Eq1 implies a constant magma.
3.  **Permutation Testing:** If Eq1 suggests the operation is a bijection (permutation) but Eq2 implies a constant result, look for counter-examples in symmetric groups.

---

### **Actionable Logic (Derivation Rules)**
*   **Rule of Expansion:** "If $Eq1: x = \text{Term}(x, y)$, and $Eq2$ is more complex than $Eq1$, substitute $x \to \text{Term}(x, y)$ into itself at least three levels deep."
*   **Rule of Explicit Mapping:** "For every step, explicitly define the mapping $\sigma = \{x \mapsto \text{subterm}_1, y \mapsto \text{subterm}_2\}$. Verify parenthesis matching strictly after every substitution."
*   **Anti-Collapse Trigger:** "If the result of a substitution is $x=y$, backtrack. Look for a 'heavy' substitution (replacing a single variable with a nested term) instead of a 'collapsing' substitution (setting $y=x$)."
*   **The Bridge Requirement:** "To link $x*y$ to $y*x$, search for a substitution that isolates a term $e$ such that $e*x = x$ and $x*e = x$."

### **Core Pillars of Equational Proof Failure**
1.  **The Semantic Trap (Model-Theoretic Hallucination):** Models abandon syntactic rewriting for "hand-wavy" structural arguments (e.g., claiming the magma is a "singleton," "trivial," or a "Boolean group"). 4.  **Missing Substitution Chains:** Failure to utilize "unobvious" compound substitutions (e.g., substituting $x \leftarrow (y * z)$ or $x \leftarrow \text{Axiom}$) which are required to bridge structurally disparate equations.

### **Critical Failure Points (Pitfalls to Avoid)**
*   **The "Triviality" Escape Hatch:** Assuming $|M|=1$ or $x=y$ just because the axiom looks "restrictive." 
*   **AST Tracking Errors:** Hallucinating the collapse of nested terms (e.g., $x * (x * x)$ becoming $x * x$) to force a match with the target.
*   **Prose-Based Bridging:** Using phrases like "through a series of substitutions..." or "it can be shown..." to skip the actual algebraic derivation.
*   **Local Optima (Functional Loops):** Repeatedly deriving that $L_x$ is a permutation or bijection without using that property to perform a specific string replacement.

---

### **Refutation Heuristics (When to stop proving/start disproving)**
*   **Structural Mismatch:** If Equation 1 is balanced (equal number of variables/ops on both sides) but Equation 2 is highly "absorbing" (many variables reduced to one), the theory is likely not trivial; look for a countermodel.
*   **Independence of Variables:** if Equation 2 introduces a variable not present in Equation 1 (and Eq 1 isn't an absorbing/constant law), the implication is likely false.
*   **Failed Triviality Test:** If the model cannot derive $x=y$ within 5 steps of "Bridge" substitutions (Axiom into Axiom), the magma is likely non-trivial.

### **Actionable Logic: The "Syntactic Grit" Protocol**

**Rule 1: Mandate "Bridge" Substitutions (Deep Self-Substitution)**
If the target is simpler than the axiom, substitute the *entire axiom* into its own variables. 
*   *Action:* If $x = f(x, y)$, substitute $y \leftarrow f(z, w)$ to create $x = f(x, f(z, w))$. This "term inflation" is often necessary before cancellation can occur.

**Rule 2: Enforce Bidirectional Search (The Mid-Point Target)**
If forward progress from $Eq1$ stalls, work backward from the RHS of $Eq2$. 
*   *Action:* Apply $Eq1$ in reverse (expansion) to $Eq2$. Aim to meet the forward derivation at a common complex intermediate term.

**Rule 3: Strict "Sub-term Paramodulation"**
Do not just substitute into the main variables ($x, y, z$). Target specific inner sub-terms for replacement.
*   *Action:* Identify a nested structure in the target and find a substitution for the axiom that makes its LHS match that specific sub-structure.

**Rule 4: Explicit Alias Management**
To prevent context-window exhaustion and mental "drift" during complex proofs, use temporary aliases.
*   *Action:* Define $A = (x * y)$. Rewrite the term as $f(A, z)$. Only expand $A$ when a cancellation with another $(x * y)$ term is possible.

**Rule 5: The "Five-Step Minimum" Exploration**
Prohibit "Impossible" or "Trivial" conclusions until a minimum of 5 distinct substitution maps have been attempted.
*   *Action:* Mandatory exploration of $x \leftarrow (x * x)$, $y \leftarrow x$, and variable swapping $(x \leftrightarrow y)$ before exiting the search.

### **Core Pillars of Refutation Strategy**
1.  **Structural Asymmetry Detection:** Analyzing syntactic mismatches (variable "ghosting," depth discrepancies, and parity imbalances) to preemptively identify unprovable implications.
2.  **Minimalist Counter-Modeling:** Prioritizing small finite magmas (Sizes 2–4) and functional abstractions over exhaustive search.
3.  **Operation Isolation:** Using projection and constant magmas to decouple variable interactions and test if an equation relies on specific, unstated dependencies (like commutativity).

---

### **Key Logical Patterns (Successful Strategies)**
*   **The Projection "Kill":** Testing $x * y = x$ (Left-Zero) or $x * y = y$ (Right-Zero). This is the highest-impact strategy for identifying equations that "absorb" variables or ignore nesting structures.
*   **Coordinate-Pair Extraction (Size 4):** For "recovery" identities (e.g., $x = (y * x) * (x * z)$), define elements as pairs $(a, b)$ and the operation as a coordinate selector (e.g., $(a, b) * (c, d) = (b, c)$). This allows $Eq1$ to rebuild a variable while $Eq2$ fails due to coordinate scrambling.
*   **Affine Linear Modeling:** Over a prime field $\mathbb{Z}_p$, define $x * y = ax + by \pmod p$. If $Eq1$ permits coefficients that satisfy the identity but $Eq2$ results in a different linear combination, the implication is false.
*   **Absorbing Blocks (Size 3):** Creating a "sink" element (e.g., $0 * x = 0$). If $Eq1$ forces a term into the sink, the remaining variables in $Eq1$ become irrelevant, providing freedom to falsify $Eq2$.

---

### **Critical Failure Points (Hallucinations to Avoid)**
*   **The Commutativity Trap:** Assuming $x * y = y * x$ or $x * (y * z) = (x * y) * z$ without explicit axioms. Most "False" implications fail precisely because the magma is non-Abelian.
*   **Depth Overreach:** Assuming a rule for a nested term (e.g., $x * (x * y)$) applies to a shallow term (e.g., $x * y$). In general magmas, the "image" of deep operations is often a restricted subset of the total domain.
*   **Ghost Variable Neglection:** Ignoring variables in $Eq2$ that do not appear in $Eq1$. $Eq1$ cannot constrain the behavior of variables it does not "see."
*   **Surjectivity Illusion:** Assuming that because $x$ can be recovered (e.g., $x = y * (y * x)$), the operation is a global permutation. It may only be a permutation on a specific orbit.

---

### **Refutation Heuristics (The Skeptic’s Trigger)**
*   **Variable Mismatch:** If $Eq2$ contains a variable absent in $Eq1$, or if one side of $Eq2$ drops a variable present on the other side, **stop proving; start refuting.**
*   **Nesting Explosion:** If $Eq1$ is shallow (1–2 layers) and $Eq2$ is deep (3+ layers), look for a coordinate-pair counter-model.
*   **Order Reversal:** If $Eq2$ swaps the left-to-right order of variables compared to $Eq1$, test a Left-Zero or Right-Zero magma immediately.
*   **Constant Row Flag:** If $Eq1$ allows the result of $(x * y)$ to be independent of $y$, but $Eq2$ requires $y$ to determine the outcome, the implication is false.

---

### **The Counter-Model Recipe (Priority Order)**
1.  **Step 1 (The 10-Second Test):** Check $x * y = x$, $x * y = y$, and $x * y = 0$.
2.  **Step 2 (The Linear Test):** Test $x * y = ax + by \pmod 3$ for $a, b \in \{0, 1, 2\}$.
3.  **Step 3 (The Coordinate Test):** Use $M = \{0, 1\}^2$ and define $(a, b) * (c, d)$ to extract internal coordinates (e.g., result is $(b, c)$).
4.  **Step 4 (The Exception Table):** Create a size-3 table of all 0s. Change one cell to "1" to break $Eq2$, then minimally change other cells to "repair" $Eq1$ until a contradiction or model is reached.
